Kinematics & Projectile Motion

Launch a projectile using 2D equations and watch position, velocity, range, and max height fall right out of the same handful of 1D equations.

The equations

Position X & Y at some time t:

\[ x(t) = v_0 \cos(\theta)\, t \qquad y(t) = v_0 \sin(\theta)\, t - \tfrac{1}{2} g t^2 \]

Horizontal (x) and Vertical (y) Velocities:

\[ v_x(t) = v_0 \cos(\theta) \qquad v_y(t) = v_0 \sin(\theta) - g t \]

Projectile: Time of Flight, Range, and Max Height:

\[ t_{\text{flight}} = \frac{2 v_0 \sin(\theta)}{g} \qquad R = \frac{v_0^2 \sin(2\theta)}{g} \qquad H = \frac{v_0^2 \sin^2(\theta)}{2g} \]

Interactive: launch it

1D kinematics, for reference

\[ v = v_0 + at \qquad x = x_0 + v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2} a t^2 \qquad v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0) \]

Projectile motion is just these same equations applied separately to the horizontal (\(a = 0\)) and vertical (\(a = -g\)) directions at once — that's why \(x(t)\) is a straight line while \(y(t)\) traces a parabola above.

Important to know about Velocity:

Velocity has Magnitude and Direction, but Speed only has Magnitude. Implying Velocity is:

  • Positive on way up: \[v = + m/s\]
  • 0 at the peak of the parabola: \[v = 0 m/s\]
  • Negative on the way down: \[v = - m/s\]
